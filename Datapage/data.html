  • In 2018, over 1 million volunteers across 22 countries participated in the International Coastal Cleanup, collecting plastic pollution along more than 22,000 miles of coastline. This massive effort underscores the global commitment to addressing ocean pollution and the role of citizen science in gathering data and removing trash from coastlines.

  • Participating in beach cleanups can lead to a change in personal behavior, encouraging individuals to reduce their plastic use...

  • Household air pollution was responsible for an estimated 3.2 million deaths per year in 2020, including over 237 000 deaths of children under the age of 5.

  • Household air pollution exposure leads to noncommunicable diseases including stroke, ischaemic heart disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and lung cancer. Global disease burden takes into account not only years of life lost to early death but also the number of years lived in poor health.

  • The burden of air pollution tends to be greater across both low and middle-income countries for two reasons: indoor pollution rates tend to be high in low-income countries due to a reliance on solid fuels for cooking, and outdoor air pollution tends to increase as countries industrialize and shift from low to middle incomes.

  • The accumulation of trash on beaches and in the ocean, including microplastics from synthetic clothing fibers, poses a significant threat to marine ecosystems. With estimates suggesting that billions of plastic microfibers are present in the ocean per square kilometer, the importance of removing every piece of trash becomes clear. This emphasizes the need for ongoing efforts to clean up existing pollution and prevent future contributions.
